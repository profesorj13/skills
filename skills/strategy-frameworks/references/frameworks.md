# Strategy Frameworks — Complete Reference

## 1. Rumelt's Kernel (Richard Rumelt — *Good Strategy Bad Strategy*, *The Crux*)

The foundational framework. Every strategic analysis starts here.

### Three components of the Kernel

1. **Diagnosis** — What's really going on? Simplify complexity into a clear problem. Requires brutal honesty — naming the uncomfortable truths the organization prefers to ignore.
2. **Guiding Policy** — The general approach to tackling the challenge. A direction, not a destination. Expressed in one sentence, not a paragraph.
3. **Coherent Actions** — Concrete steps that reinforce each other. They don't compete for resources or contradict. "Coherent" is the key word — each action should amplify the others.

### The Crux (*The Crux*, 2022)

From rock climbing: the crux is the hardest move on the route. If you solve it, the rest becomes manageable.

**Questions to find the crux:**
- What is the hardest challenge we face?
- If we solved only this problem, would everything else unblock?
- What are we avoiding?

**Common trap:** Dispersing energy on easy tasks to avoid the hard one. The crux is usually uncomfortable — a conversation, a decision, a truth nobody wants to accept.

### Signals of "bad strategy" (Rumelt)

- **Fluff:** Inflated language that simulates strategic thinking without real content.
- **Failure to diagnose:** Jumping to solutions without understanding the problem.
- **Confusing goals with strategy:** "Our strategy is to grow" says nothing.
- **Objectives without logic:** A list of desirable things with no coherence between them.

## 2. Choice Cascade (Roger Martin — *Playing to Win*, with A.G. Lafley)

Framework for business decisions. Five cascading questions — each conditions the next.

### The five questions

1. **What is our winning aspiration?** — Not generic. Specific about what "winning" means for this product/business.
2. **Where will we play?** — Customer segments, geographies, channels, product categories. Choosing means excluding.
3. **How will we win there?** — Concrete competitive advantage. Why do they choose us over anyone else?
4. **What capabilities do we need?** — The capabilities that must exist for the strategy to work.
5. **What management systems do we need?** — Processes, metrics, organizational structures.

### Validation tests (Martin)

**Discomfort test:** "If your strategy doesn't make you uncomfortable about what you're leaving out, it's not a strategy."

**Falsifiability test:** "What would have to be true for this strategy to work?" Articulate assumptions explicitly. If you can't, you're guessing.

**Choice test:** If all competitors could adopt your same "strategy," it's not a strategy — it's an industry practice.

## 3. 7 Powers (Hamilton Helmer — *7 Powers: The Foundations of Business Strategy*)

Framework for evaluating whether a business has (or can build) durable competitive advantage. Without at least one "power," you're competing on execution alone — and that erodes over time.

### The 7 Powers

1. **Scale Economies** — Unit costs decrease with volume. The larger player always has a cost advantage over the smaller one. (Ex: Intel, Walmart)
2. **Network Economies** — Product value increases with each additional user. Winner-take-most. (Ex: LinkedIn, WhatsApp, Visa)
3. **Counter-Positioning** — A business model the incumbent can't copy without damaging their current business. (Ex: Vanguard index funds vs. active managers, Netflix streaming vs. cable)
4. **Switching Costs** — The cost (time, money, effort) of switching to a competitor is high. Lock-in. (Ex: SAP, Salesforce, any SaaS with accumulated data)
5. **Branding** — Durable attribution of higher value that justifies premium pricing or preference. (Ex: Tiffany, Apple, Hermès)
6. **Cornered Resource** — Preferential/exclusive access to a valuable resource: patents, key talent, unique data, prime location, exclusive relationships. (Ex: DeBeers with diamonds, Pixar with talent)
7. **Process Power** — Embedded organizational processes that competitors can't easily replicate. Built over years of iteration. (Ex: Toyota Production System)

### How to use

For any strategic option, ask:
- Which of the 7 powers are we building toward?
- If none, it's operational improvement, not strategy.
- Which power is most accessible from our current position?

### Timing

Helmer distinguishes between powers that build early (counter-positioning, cornered resource) and those that build with scale (scale economies, network economies, branding). Process power requires years.

## 4. Integral Validation Test (synthesis)

Run every proposed strategy through these filters:

- [ ] **Does it identify a specific challenge?** (Not "the market is big" — what's the obstacle?)
- [ ] **Does it make a clear choice?** (What are we NOT doing? If it doesn't hurt, you didn't choose.)
- [ ] **Are the actions coherent?** (Do they reinforce each other or compete for resources?)
- [ ] **Does it build toward a power?** (Helmer — or is it just execution?)
- [ ] **Is it falsifiable?** (Martin — what would make it fail? What has to be true?)
- [ ] **Does it attack the crux?** (Rumelt — are we on the most important problem?)

## 5. Cross-cutting Principles

### On diagnosis
- You can't solve a problem you don't name. Force the uncomfortable truth.
- Distinguish symptoms from causes. "I don't have time" is a symptom. The cause is lack of prioritization.
- Most "problems" are decisions that weren't made.

### On choices
- Strategy = choosing, and choosing = sacrificing.
- "We do everything" is the absence of strategy.
- What you don't do is more important than what you do.
- If everyone is comfortable, probably nothing real was chosen.

### On execution
- Coherence > quantity. Three aligned actions > ten disconnected ones.
- The crux first. Don't disperse energy on the easy stuff.
- Measure before optimizing. Without data, there's no improvement.

### What is NOT strategy
- Goals ("grow 3x")
- Project lists (without coherent logic)
- Vision (destination ≠ navigation)
- Infrastructure (tools are means, not ends)
- Consensus (avoiding conflict doesn't produce clarity)

## 6. Expanded Author References

- **Richard Rumelt** — *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* (2011), *The Crux* (2022). For diagnosis and focus.
- **Roger Martin** — *Playing to Win* (2013), *A New Way to Think* (2022). For choices and falsifiability.
- **Hamilton Helmer** — *7 Powers* (2016). For durable competitive advantage.
- **Patrick Lencioni** — *The Advantage* (2012). For organizational alignment. Key insight: without trust and productive conflict in the leadership team, there are no real strategic decisions.
- **Rita McGrath** — *The End of Competitive Advantage* (2013). For fast-changing markets. Transient advantages instead of durable ones.
- **W. Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne** — *Blue Ocean Strategy* (2004/2015). For creating new markets instead of fighting in existing ones.
